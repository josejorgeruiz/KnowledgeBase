## Excerpt
A practical framework for aligning sales talent to revenue strategy by diagnosing five distinct **Sources of Revenue** and defining the sales patterns each source is most likely to reward.

## Abstract
Many sales teams hire to a mythic archetype—the extroverted Hunter-Closer—and then wonder why results vary by market, stage, customer context, and offer. The **Luks Prisma Sales Model** resolves that mismatch by separating three things that organizations often blur.

The **Model** is the full logic system. It begins with the **Five Sources of Revenue**, which diagnose the kind of revenue work a role is responsible for. Only then does it move to the six Prisma dimensions, which define the behavioral pattern the work is most likely to reward.

The **Profile** is the specific pattern across those six dimensions—**Type, Style, Intensity, Community, Role, and Drive**—that best fits a given kind of revenue work.

The **Assessment** is the diagnostic instrument used to evaluate a person against that profile so leaders can make better decisions in hiring, deployment, development, and team design.

The five Sources—maintaining current revenue, expanding within current accounts, acquiring new customers with current offers, expanding current customers with new offers, and creating revenue with new customers and new offers—carry different degrees of uncertainty, trust demand, cycle length, and collaboration need. The six dimensions capture how a seller tends to pursue work and make trade-offs under pressure. This paper explains the distinction between Model, Profile, and Assessment, shows how profile leanings shift by Source of Revenue, and translates the framework into decisions on role design, hiring, account assignment, handoffs, metrics, and development.

## Introduction
Ask ten executives for the “best sales profile” and most will describe a charismatic Hunter who always beelines to the close. That profile does exist, and it thrives in some conditions. But when the revenue work actually demands patience, trust continuity, systems thinking, or heavy internal coordination, that same pattern can work against the goal.

That is why the **Luks Prisma Sales Model** starts with strategy, not stereotype. It does not begin by labeling people. It begins by diagnosing the revenue work.

The first part of the Model is the **Five Sources of Revenue**. These Sources clarify what kind of revenue responsibility the role actually carries. The second part of the Model is the Prisma logic of fit, expressed through six dimensions. Together, these dimensions form the **Luks Prisma Sales Profile** for a role or describe the operating pattern of a person. The **Luks Prisma Sales Assessment** is then used to evaluate how closely a person matches the profile the role requires.

This distinction matters. The Model is the full method. The Profile is the target pattern of fit inside that method. The Assessment is the instrument used to gather evidence and support judgment about fit.

The five Sources of Revenue are not merely growth ambitions. They are distinct forms of commercial work. Maintaining current revenue is different from expanding within current accounts. Acquiring new customers with current offers is different from expanding current customers with new offers. Creating revenue with new customers and new offers is different again. Each Source carries its own risk, tempo, relationship logic, and consequences when mismatch is ignored. That is why each Source tends to reward a different sales Profile.

## The Five Sources of Revenue
**Source One — Maintain Revenue from Current Customers.** This is continuity work. The task is to protect the base through relationship quality, reliability, responsiveness, and full-cycle follow-through.

**Source Two — Add Revenue from Current Customers.** This is expansion work inside an existing relationship. The task is to deepen share of wallet without weakening trust.

**Source Three — Add Revenue from New Customers with Current Offers.** This is acquisition work in a proven context. The task is to win new logos where the offer is understood and speed, opening, and conversion matter.

**Source Four — Add Revenue from Current Customers with New Offers.** This is trust-extension work. The relationship exists, but the offer is new, so the seller must help the customer move into unfamiliar territory without forcing the sale.

**Source Five — Add Revenue from New Customers with New Offers.** This is market-making work. Both the audience and the offer carry uncertainty, so the seller must help shape demand while protecting credibility and coordinating learning across the organization.

## The Six Dimensions of the Luks Prisma Sales Profile
The **Luks Prisma Sales Profile** describes a sales pattern across six continua:

**Type (Hunter-Farmer).** Hunters are oriented to opening demand and creating new relationships. Farmers focus on nurturing, shaping, and expanding existing relationships.

**Style (Empirical-Methodical).** Empirical sellers work through observation, experience, and experimentation. Methodical sellers work through structure, planning, and disciplined follow-through.

**Intensity (Sprinter-Marathoner).** Sprinters optimize for speed and short-cycle effectiveness. Marathoners favor thoroughness, patience, and long-cycle effectiveness.

**Community (Individual-Group).** Individual-leaning sellers trust personal initiative. Group-leaning sellers anchor effort in team coordination and shared commitments.

**Role (Leader-Collaborator).** Leader-leaning sellers drive results through orchestration and the work of others. Collaborator-leaning sellers drive results more directly through their own hands-on contribution.

**Drive (Closer-Promoter).** Closers prioritize getting deals over the line. Promoters prioritize building durable relationships that compound into future wins.

Two application rules matter. First, there are no absolutes. Most successful sellers blend both poles over time. Second, some dimensions move more with the Source of Revenue than others. **Type** and **Drive** tend to shift most clearly with the Source. **Intensity** and **Role** often depend more on cycle length, product complexity, and the specific mandate of the role.

## How the Profile Shifts by Source of Revenue

### Source One — Maintain Revenue from Current Customers
For revenue protection, the pattern leans Farmer and Promoter. The work requires preserving satisfaction, anticipating needs, and placing relationship continuity above any single deal. Community leans Group because continuity depends on coordinated delivery across the full request-to-delivery cycle. The test is not only whether the seller can serve the customer well, but whether the seller can protect trust across organizational seams.

### Source Two — Add Revenue from Current Customers
To expand within existing accounts, Type still leans Farmer, but Drive brings in more Closer energy. The work is no longer only to preserve trust. It is to turn trust into relationship economics. Style leans Methodical because cadence, coverage, preparation, and disciplined follow-up matter more than charisma. Community remains Group because expansion depends on promises the wider organization must keep.

### Source Three — Add Revenue from New Customers with Current Offers
This is the most natural arena for a Hunter-Closer pattern. The role is to win new customers where the product and market are already proven. Style should be balanced: empirical instinct to open doors, method to create repeatability. Community can lean more Individual without becoming destructive, especially in field sales or fast-moving acquisition environments. Even so, leaders must design clean handoffs so Source Three energy does not damage Source One and Source Two relationship work after the account enters the base.

### Source Four — Add Revenue from Current Customers with New Offers
Here the seller must extend trust into unfamiliar territory. Type should balance Hunter and Farmer because the role requires both opportunity creation and care for an existing relationship. Drive leans Promoter rather than hard Closer because over-closing can weaken confidence when the customer is still learning the offer. Style leans Empirical because ambiguity, iteration, and learning cycles are part of the work. Community leans Group because product, implementation, service, and commercial teams often need to move together.

### Source Five — Add Revenue from New Customers with New Offers
This is the highest-ambiguity Source. Type again balances Hunter and Farmer because the seller must open relationships while also building trust from the ground up. Drive leans Promoter. Style leans Empirical. Community leans strongly Group because collaboration with product, implementation, and leadership is continuous. Source Five profiles should be understood as market-building profiles, not merely selling profiles.

## The Difference Between Model, Profile, and Assessment
The distinction can now be stated clearly.

The **Luks Prisma Sales Model** is the full framework. It diagnoses the revenue work through the Five Sources of Revenue and defines fit through the six Prisma dimensions.

The **Luks Prisma Sales Profile** is the resulting pattern of fit across the six dimensions for a role, team, or person.

The **Luks Prisma Sales Assessment** is the diagnostic instrument used to evaluate a person against that profile so the organization can make more coherent decisions.

The Model tells you how to think.  
The Profile tells you what pattern the work requires.  
The Assessment helps you determine whether a specific person fits that pattern.

## Practical Applications

### Hiring and Selection
Begin every search by naming the Source of Revenue the role must primarily serve over the next 12 to 24 months. Use that Source to set default leanings across the six dimensions, paying closest attention to Type and Drive. Then confirm Style and Community against the account context and handoff structure. For Intensity, map the sales cycle and the consequence of error: short transactional cycles reward Sprinter leanings, while long complex pursuits reward Marathoner patience. Role should reflect the mandate: if the work is to orchestrate others, lean Leader; if the work is to personally prospect and convert, lean Collaborator. Use structured interviews, simulations, and evidence to assess how candidates handle the actual dilemmas of the Source.

### Team Design and Handoffs
Mixed revenue portfolios rarely thrive with one monolithic profile. Design deliberately to the seams. Source Three openers should not automatically own Source One continuity work. Source Two expanders should not always be asked to perform Source Five market creation. A useful design rule is simple: the person whose native pattern best matches the next revenue task should own the relationship at that moment. This keeps one Source from quietly damaging another.

### Account Assignment and Pipeline Governance
Assign opportunities by fit, not fairness alone. Short-cycle new-logo pushes belong to Hunter-Closer Sprinter patterns. Multi-stakeholder pursuits with trust and learning demands call for more balanced profiles, often with stronger Group and Promoter leanings. In-base expansion work usually favors Methodical Farmers who can orchestrate delivery and earn the right to ask for more. Review progression with two lenses: velocity where cycles are short, and quality of learning, trust, and stakeholder expansion where cycles are long.

### Incentives and Metrics
Compensation should echo the Source of Revenue. For Source Three work, weight new-logo count and cycle time. For Source One and Source Two, weight retention, satisfaction, renewal quality, and depth of expansion. For Source Four and Source Five, recognize validated learning, adoption, multi-function coordination, and milestone progress in addition to revenue. Tie team incentives to Group-leaning work when delivery, adoption, and continuity matter.

### Development and Mobility
Treat the six dimensions as a developmental map, not a verdict. Many sellers can widen their range with coaching, design, and time. Farmers can strengthen closing discipline. Hunters can learn to steward trust. The goal is range with clarity: make the Source of Revenue explicit, define the required Profile, and then help the seller develop without forcing a false identity.

### Product and Sales-Cycle Intensity
Two variables often shape Intensity and Role more than the Source itself: product complexity and cycle length. Short-cycle transactional offerings tend to reward Sprinter patterns. Complex, multi-month pursuits demand Marathoner patience and often more Leader expression to coordinate teams and stakeholders. Anchor these choices in the buying reality of the work rather than personal preference.

## Implementation Steps
First, map your revenue portfolio by **Source** for the coming year so roles reflect real work rather than generic sales ambition. Second, define the default **Profile** for each role across the six dimensions, with special attention to Type and Drive. Third, design explicit handoff rules in the customer journey so Source-specific work has clear ownership. Fourth, use the **Assessment** to evaluate candidates and incumbents against the real tensions of the Source, not against a generic sales stereotype. Finally, measure outcomes that correspond to the work: continuity and renewal in the base, expansion depth in current accounts, conversion and velocity in new-logo acquisition, and learning, adoption, and trust extension in new-offer work.

## Conclusion
Sales excellence is not a single personality. It is a match between the nature of the revenue work and the human pattern assigned to it.

The **Luks Prisma Sales Model** gives leaders the full method: diagnose the work through the **Five Sources of Revenue**, define fit through the **Luks Prisma Sales Profile**, and evaluate people through the **Luks Prisma Sales Assessment**. When those three are kept distinct, sales stops being a mythology of charisma and becomes a discipline of design.

Done well, this approach improves fit, sharpens hiring, reduces internal friction, strengthens customer continuity, and makes revenue more coherent because the right person is doing the right kind of work at the right time.
